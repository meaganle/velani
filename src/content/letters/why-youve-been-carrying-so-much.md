It didn't happen all at once. That's what makes it hard to see.

I want to tell you something about how this happens to us. Not what it is. How it happens. Because I don't think it arrives like a wave. I think it arrives like weather — slowly, gradually, so incrementally that by the time you notice how heavy everything feels, you can't quite remember when it started feeling this way.

For me, it started with being good at things.

I was the one who followed through. Who remembered. Who made sure things didn't fall apart. At work, I was the person you could count on to hold the details, track the moving parts, keep the momentum going even when everything felt uncertain. At home, I was the one who noticed what needed doing before it became a problem.

And because I was good at it, more came.

Not maliciously. Not even consciously, I think. It's just what happens when you become the person who handles things. The circle of responsibility expands quietly. A little more at work. A little more at home. A little more from the people who love you and know you'll figure it out.

And for a long time, I didn't mind. I was capable. I was competent. I liked being useful.

But somewhere in there — I can't tell you exactly when — something shifted.

The work kept moving. The family was healthy. There were goals and plans and things to look forward to. From the outside, nothing was wrong.

But I started noticing that I was exhausted in a way that didn't make sense. Not from anything specific. Just from the weight of all of it. The managing. The remembering. The being the person that everything filtered through.

And the version of me that used to exist underneath all of that — the one who read things just because they were interesting, who started things without knowing if they'd work, who had opinions about small beautiful things and time to pursue them — she had gotten very quiet.

I didn't lose her dramatically. She just got crowded out.

By the career I cared about. By the family I love. By the responsibilities I chose and would choose again. By the life that, on paper, was everything I wanted.

Life became all responsibility. And there was less and less room left for the parts of me that weren't useful to anyone.

I've been thinking about this a lot lately, talking to women who are carrying versions of the same thing. And what I keep noticing is how rarely we name it. We assume it's normal. We assume it's the price of a full life. We assume we just need to manage better, plan better, optimise better.

But I don't think it's a planning problem.

I think it's an accumulation problem. And the first step isn't a system. It's just seeing it clearly — maybe for the first time — and saying: *this is a lot. I have been carrying a lot. And that is allowed to be true.*

That's what this letter is for. That's what Velani is for.

Not to give you more to carry. To help you figure out what you might finally be allowed to put down.

One clear move at a time.
